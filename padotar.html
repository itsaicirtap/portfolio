Context

This project, part of my master's thesis, focuses on a mobile app aimed at addressing the increasing number of abandoned animals, which burdens animal shelters and protection associations. Unlike social media platforms used by shelters, this app categorizes content for a more intuitive user experience, facilitating the adoption process, enhancing communication with shelters, and providing educational content for pet owners.

Padotar is a fictitious shelter created purely for the project. The conception of the mobile app was restricted to the process of developing a digital product from a design perspective and was not implemented after the evaluation of the interactive hi-fi prototype. Bridging the Gap

Two interviews were conducted with individuals working in animal welfare institutions. During these interviews, participants shared problems and needs, providing valuable information for the project's development. In total, twelve questions were asked, covering the general context of the shelter, the adoption process, the impact of new media, and the implementation of alternative digital tools.

Subsequently, I conducted brief, informal conversations with two potencial users that provided valuable insights that helped define functionalities and create user personas reflecting real experiences.

Gathering Findings

Summary of findings identified through literature review and interviews:

Size, behavior and personality are determining factors in adoption.

Give visibility to sponsorships, donations, members and volunteering.

Clearing up common doubts among pet owners and educating potential adopters are key to successful pet placements.

Provide news about the shelter and the activities carried out by them.

Solidarity sales contribute to the financial sustainability of the shelter.

Maintain a coherent aesthetic and positive speech.

Branding

Padotar

With the app structure finalized, I turned my attention to its visual identity. How could the app effectively communicate the brand personality of Padotar?

The first step was to select the name of our fictitious shelter. Since it is a shelter for animals looking for a family and the comfort of a home, I combined the words 'para' (for) and 'adotar' (to adopt). These form the word 'padotar', which, coincidentally, results in a sound effect similar to their hurried pronunciation. I then began experimenting with the graphic brand and once the brand was defined, I introduced the typography, giving rise to the logo.

Padotar's brand image is defined by a symbol depicting a dog and a cat inside a shelter, representing the most common animals found in these institutions. The main color, orange, was chosen for its contrast with other elements, conveying the shelter's values of joy, trust, strength, and ambition. The logo features a simple, bold sans-serif font, chosen to visually balance the design and emphasize the shelter's name.

Designing for Consistency

To enhance user experience and maintain design consistency, we're integrating the orange from the logo into other visual components. Complementing this orange, I chose a pastel shade, an analogous color, aiming to evoke serenity and comfort. Black text with an RGB value below 100% ensures readability without excessive contrast that might strain users' eyes during extended use.

Prioritizing both simplicity and readability, especially for users with visual impairments, dyslexia, or cognitive difficulties, I selected two sans-serif fonts. Futura, used previously in the logo, is applied to the titles, while Avenir Next is used for all other written content. This distinction aids in differentiating headings from paragraphs.

Prototype

Visualizing Concepts

To organize Padotar's app content in line with project goals, I developed a low-fidelity wireframe using basic geometric shapes and minimal details to emphasize flow and features.

Paper Prototype

Here, it becomes essential to focus on the interface design to visualize the complete user journey and explore methods for maximizing the intuitiveness of the pet search and adoption process. This initial exploration allows us to gain a foundational understanding of how the interface will function as users engage in specific tasks.

To assess the intuitiveness of the flows, I invited two individuals to analyze and provide feedback on a prototype. Following their insights, I opted to simplify and streamline some of the features:

This feature is not a current priority and can be deprioritized for now.

Remove redundant options on "What are you looking for?" since they're all available in the menu.

Improve the quiz experience by indicating progress through "Question X of X" for clarity. Avoid asking too many questions to maintain user engagement.

The adoption section features an extensive page of filters to assist in finding the perfect pet, rendering the search bar redundant. Users can still choose the "See all" option to view all available pets without specific filters.

Consider removing features such as favorites, login, and profile since user accounts would only serve the purpose of maintaining a favorites list. Additionally, remove app settings as all customizable features have been eliminated.

Combine general articles, training, and news into a single tab, introducing a search filter to compile similar information and reduce menu options.

With the reduction of tabs to about five elements, explore placing the menu at the bottom of the screen for improved accessibility.

The objective of these tests was to identify usability problems that users may encounter in the interface and receive feedback with the aim of improving it. Six participants were recruited and all performed the same tasks to allow comparison.

Priority Revisions

Since it wasn't possible to implement all suggestions, prioritizing the findings became necessary. To achieve this, I employed the Nielsen Scale to categorize each suggestion. This helped me prioritize the four most critical issues consistently mentioned by multiple users.

Is there anything I should improve?
Increase menu button padding for easier navigation.
We should be able to see all adoptable animals initially, without mandatory filters.
Move FAQ to "About Us" followed by the contact form for further clarification.
Pre-fill adoption form fields like animal type and name for user convenience.

Conclusion

Reflection & Next Steps

Designed to streamline pet adoption, Padotar offers a promising approach to enhancing shelter operations and increasing adoption rates. This mobile app offers a streamlined adoption process, fosters connections between potential adopters and shelters, and empowers users with valuable resources for pet care.

In order to prioritize the user experience, an iterative approach was adopted based on the User-Centered Design (UCD) process, where key usability issues that could hinder user adoption were identified early on. This method, I believe, was highly effective in ensuring that the overall layout of content is effective and functional. If you're interested in analyzing the details of the design process, access this link for my thesis.

Inclue: An experience for everyone!

Inclue is a project about digital accessibility developed within the scope of the Master's program in New Media and Web Practices, together with 3 colleagues. Our objective is, through interviews with real testimonies, to explore what it's like to live in the digital age with disabilities that pose digital accessibility challenges. The aim is to educate and sensitize students and future professionals in the area.

We created several versions of the same video, simulating accessibility challenges for the user. After watching it, you will be able to choose a 'character' and watch the video from the perspective of their disability. That way, you will understand the challenges faced and have access to excerpts from the interview and images provided by the selected user. On the project page, we present the ideal version of this video, accessible to everyone.

It is essentially an interactive web documentary about accessibility and digital inclusion, where users can choose paths that reflect various perspectives of reality, according to a disability (visual, hearing, etc.). The user has an exploratory stance towards the content, exploring predefined options that connect to multimedia content.

Unfortunately, due to time constraints, we were unable to complete this project. However, I considered the concept relevant to share, and what follows is a sketch of our ideas.

Project Overview

Timeline

Mar 8 - Jun 15

Problem

Interface developers do not have a high degree of responsibility and attention to the accessibility of digital platforms. Often, people with disabilities are unable to access content due to the lack of adequate resources for their condition.

Solution

Through an interactive platform designed according to the different types of disabilities, the common user will be able to access the content, as if they had the disability, and this will allow an “immersion” in the reality of the problem.

Research

Interviews

We decided to conduct interviews with two types of users: computer engineers or programmers (ranging from students to senior developers) and professionals in the field of digital content creation. These two profiles would help us better understand the digital product market and why accessibility is still lacking, both in interfaces and content.

We created a brief script to ensure that all group members conducted the same interview. Each member did two interviews, which took place in person or remotely. Below is the script used:

  • Have you studied or taken courses related to accessibility?
  • How much do you know about disabilities when it comes to accessibility?
  • Do you know how a screen reader works?
  • Do you consider accessibility in your work?
  • Do you know the daily life of a person with a disability?
  • Do you think there is a lack of awareness about this subject?

Afinity Map

After completing and transcribing the interviews, we proceeded to analyze and group the responses. Each group member selected pertinent excerpts from the obtained answers, placing them on sticky notes in Miro. Subsequently, we conducted a brief review of similarities, which, when grouped together, formed our affinity map.

Findings

Main findings identified:

  • There is a tenuous understanding of what a screen reader is, with the majority having never seen one in use;
  • While awareness has improved, there's still a significant lack of understanding on the subject;
  • It seems that companies tend to prioritize content for the common user, and accessibility is not considered a priority;
  • While participants are aware of various disabilities, none of them are familiar with the daily challenges faced by individuals with disabilities.

Persona

After understanding and evaluating the common points of each interview, we created a persona, which was entirely based on our interviews and respective findings.

Define & Ideate

Content & Flow

To start producing content for our project, we thought of some video solutions. We decided that we would do interviews on a white background in the studio with people with disabilities who could tell us about the difficulties they face in the digital world. We contacted some institutions to ask for interviewees and got three contacts:

  • André Almeida: deaf (Interpreter required);
  • André Muricy: colorblind;
  • Rita Pereira: dyslexic.

We decided to try to create as much content as possible and build a prototype around it, adapting what we could.

What we aim for:

  • On the first screen, a fixed background image or just the logo will appear on a white background and when scrolling, the introductory video will appear. This will contain the information that it is an immersive experience, with sound and that you can use the keyboard to navigate. It's a kind of on board;
  • When scrolling, we come across the third screen, which consists of the split screen where the user will choose one of the experiences. Here we will place conceptual photos, creating a surprise factor and arousing the user's curiosity to want to know that testimony just because of what the image suggests and the brief description of it;
  • Once the experience is selected, an adapted video appears depending on the choice made. Therefore, this fourth screen is the presentation of a testimony with the difficulty represented in the video. To access this content, the user simply needs to scroll after the adapted video has finished;
  • We agreed that it would not be aesthetically appealing and that it could make it difficult to read certain content if the navigation bar were present throughout the experiences. However, navigation can be done using the links in the footer or the 'back' and 'next experience' buttons, which will be found at the end of the page;
  • We considered it relevant to add the "About us" page where the team members will be presented, a "About the project" page delving deeper into our concept and objectives, and finally, "Know more" so that we can provide the contacts of the associations.

We intend to create an interface that works specifically based on scrolling and content that appears with subtle effects throughout the page. With this, we intend to make these stories known with a small differentiation, which consists of exposing the problems and possible solutions suggested by those who really have difficulties with digital accessibility.

Branding

Branding

Now that we have refined the overall structure of the app, we are focusing on creating Inclue's branding to effectively communicate the brand personality through the website.

Logo Design

Inclue was born from the search for a concise name that encapsulates the essence of the documentary, embodying both its form and content. The result emerged from the fusion of 'inclusive' and 'clue,' symbolizing not only inclusion and understanding of diverse experiences and challenges but also the interactive experience designed to offer users valuable insights on improving digital accessibility.
After selecting the name, we began experimenting in Adobe Illustrator, exploring simpler options to make the union of the two concepts perceptible and more daring ones that took the concepts as a whole and explored the graphic component.

From the options above, we decided to opt for the one in the bottom right corner.
We presented our logo proposal to 36 people unfamiliar with our project's purpose. The conclusion was that 100% of respondents understood the represented word on the first try. A recurring observation was that it requires some concentration to perceive, but it is always correct.
With that in mind, we made minor adjustments to the letters that people found most challenging to decipher. Without deviating from the fundamental idea of constructing the word with geometric shapes, we incorporated miniature elements to enhance the characteristics and recognition of each letter in the alphabet. Although subtle, the small openings (visible in the letters N, C, and U) facilitate readability while preserving the logo's initial aesthetic.

Our logo was constructed based on the Roboto font. Each letter of the word 'Inclue' underwent a geometrization process, incorporating various geometric shapes with subtle transparencies and overlaps. Given that the word itself conveys the essence of inclusion, the diverse shapes symbolize unity and strength, aligning with our primary goal of promoting awareness through an universally accessible experience. By integrating these geometric elements into the chosen typography, we imbue it with a distinctive character that can also serve as versatile graphic elements to complement our identity in the future.

We conducted a color contrast test on the logo using the Dalton Chrome plugin. This confirmed that there would be ample contrast between the selected colors, ensuring easy distinguishability for individuals with color blindness. In the image above, you can visualize this color conversion, depicting the most prevalent types of color blindness.

Style Guide

To resonate with a youthful audience, our project's image is vibrant and brimming with personality. The use of blue aims to convey a sense of security, stability, and calmness. Yellow, in contrast, brings vivacity and energy and purple, symbolizing wisdom and knowledge, is often employed by brands that value humanity as a whole.
The color selection played a pivotal role in our project's overall development. Our research underscored the importance of choosing colors with accessibility in mind, emphasizing the play with contrast to enhance distinguishability.
The selected colors exhibit excellent contrast, ensuring easy differentiation for individuals with color blindness. While Tritanopia is less common, the specific shade of purple we chose is visually contrasting for those with this form of color blindness.
The Roboto font used in the logo will also take a prominent place on the website. Its simplicity and sans-serif design aim to facilitate reading for individuals with reduced vision, dyslexia, or cognitive and neurological difficulties.

Test

Usability Test

In order to address questions about our website, we conducted usability tests with four individuals. They interacted with what we consider a demo version of the prototype, which is still in progress.
Given the narrative and exploratory nature of our project, we adapted the usability tests to gather opinions after users explored the website. Unlike typical task-oriented tests, we allowed users complete freedom to explore. This approach helped us assess how easily they navigated and found information, whether they grasped the main objective of the project, and identified areas that didn't work or could be improved.
Users were given the option to provide feedback during or after the test.

Priority Revisions

Most frequent suggestions:

  • The navigation bar should be hidden on the first page to emphasize that the project is an interactive documentary;
  • Add more arrows indicating the path to follow, helping users understand what to do after watching a video;
  • Find a solution for integrating videos on the website that maintains the visual integrity of the documentary. YouTube recommendations, file names, and starting frames can hinder the immersive experience;
  • Diversify the thumbnails, making each one unique to the information it represents;
  • Include a clearer message in disability immersion videos, guiding users to rewatch even though they may appear identical, to ensure they understand the interactive nature;
  • Consider reducing the number of videos on André Almeida's page for a more streamlined experience;
  • Enhance the clarity and cohesion of written information, addressing font size for improved readability;
  • Conclude the experience with suggestions for improving digital accessibility, presented in written form.

Reflection & Next Steps

When we chose the concept of inclusion and accessibility for our project, we had the opportunity to delve into this topic and communicate with people who taught us a lot and made us think. Overall, we are satisfied with the final result of the project, however, we would like to see the website's final version implemented, focusing on its interactivity, as it is not finished. If I were to take this project further, I would:

1. Re-test

Address identified issues in the prototype and conduct a retest to ensure the improvements are effective.

2. Design Handoff

Present the finalized design to stakeholders for approval and hand off the approved design to developers for implementation.

Inclue: An experience for everyone!

Inclue is a project about digital accessibility developed within the scope of the Master in New Media and Web Practices, together with 3 colleagues. Our objective is, through interviews with real testimonies, of what it is like to live in the digital age with some disability that makes digital accessibility difficult, to educate and sensitize students and future professionals in the area.

We created several versions of the same video, in which we present a completely inaccessible version to the user. After viewing it, you will be able to choose a "character" and view the video from the point of view of his disability. That said, you will realize the problem in question and have access to excerpts from the interview and images provided by the selected user. On the page about the project, we present the ideal version of this video, accessible to everyone.

It is essentially an interactive web documentary about accessibility and digital inclusion, where the paths that users can choose reflect different perceptions of reality, according to a disability (visual, hearing, etc.). The user has an exploratory stance towards the content, accessing pre-existing options that make connections with media files.

Due to time constraints this project was not finished, therefore, this is a sketch of our ideas.

Project Overview

Problem

Interface developers do not have a high degree of responsibility and attention to the accessibility of digital platforms. Often, people with disabilities are unable to access content due to the lack of adequate resources for their condition.

Solution

Through an interactive platform designed according to the different types of disabilities, the common user will be able to access the content, as if they had the disability, and this will allow an “immersion” in the reality of the problem.

Research

Interviews

We decided that we would conduct interviews with two types of users: computer engineers or programmers (from students to senior developers) and with professionals in the field of digital content creation. These two profiles would help us better understand the digital product market and why accessibility is still lacking, both in interfaces and content.

We created a brief script so that all group members could do the same interview. Each member did two interviews, which took place in person or remotely. Below is the script used:

  • Do you study or have studied the subject of accessibility?
  • How much do you know about disabilities when it comes to accessibility?
  • Do you know how a screen reader works?
  • Do you consider accessibility in your work?
  • Do you know the daily life of a person with a disability?
  • Do you think there is a lack of awareness about this subject?

Afinity Map

After completing and transcribing the interviews, we moved on to the stage of analyzing and grouping the responses. Each group member selected relevant excerpts from the answers obtained, placing them on sticky notes in Miro. Afterwards, we carried out a brief review of similarities, which when grouped together, resulted in our affinity map.

Findings

Main findings identified:

  • They seem to have a very tenuous notion of what a screen reader is, and it is important to note that the majority have never seen one in use;
  • Everyone agrees that although it has improved, there is still a great lack of awareness on the subject;
  • It seems that companies still prefer to produce content for the common user, with accessibility not being a priority;
  • They all know different disabilities, however, none of them know the daily challenges of those with one.

Persona

After understanding and evaluating the common points of each interview, we created a persona, which was entirely based on our interviews and respective findings.

Define & Ideate

Content & Flow

To start producing content for our project, we thought of some video solutions. We decided that we would do interviews on a white background in the studio with people with disabilities who could tell us about the difficulties they face in the digital world. We contacted some institutions to ask for interviewees and got three contacts:

  • André Almeida: deaf (Interpreter required);
  • André Muricy: colorblind;
  • Rita Pereira: dyslexic.

We decided to try to create as much content as possible and build a prototype around it, adapting what we could.

What we aim for:

  • On the first screen, a fixed background image or just the logo will appear on a white background and when scrolling, the introductory video will appear. This will contain the information that it is an immersive experience, with sound and that you can use the keyboard to navigate. It's a kind of on board;
  • When scrolling, we come across the third screen, which consists of the split screen where the user will choose one of the experiences. Here we will place conceptual photos, creating a surprise factor and arousing the user's curiosity to want to know that testimony just because of what the image suggests and the brief description of it;
  • Once the experience is selected, an adapted video appears depending on the choice made. Therefore, this fourth screen is the presentation of a testimony with the difficulty represented in the video. To access this content, the user simply needs to scroll after the adapted video has finished;
  • We agreed that it would not be aesthetically appealing and that it could make it difficult to read certain content if the navigation bar was present throughout the experiences. However, navigation can be done using the links in the footer or the back and next experience buttons, which will be found at the end of the page;
  • We considered it relevant to add the "About us" page where the team members will be presented, a "About the project" page delving deeper into our concept and objectives, and finally, "Know more" so that we can provide the contacts of the associations.

We intend to create an interface that works specifically based on scrolling and content that appears with subtle effects throughout the page. With this, we intend to make these stories known with a small differentiation, which consists of exposing the problems and possible solutions suggested by those who really have difficulties with digital accessibility.

Branding

Branding

Now that we have refined the overall structure of the app we focused on creating Inclue's branding to effectively communicate the brand personality through the website.

Logo Design

Inclue was born from the search for a short name, which symbolized the idea of the documentary in its form and content. The result arose from the combination of the words "inclusive" and "clue", which means inclusion and knowledge of the different experiences and challenges associated with them, and the interactive experience from which the user will leave with several clues on how to improve digital accessibility.
After selecting the name, we began experimenting in Adobe Illustrator. From simpler options where we try to make the union of the two concepts perceptible, to more daring ones, in which we take the concepts as a whole and explore the graphic component.

From the options above we decided to opt for the one in the bottom right corner.
We presented our logo proposal to 36 people who were unaware of the purpose of our project and came to the conclusion that 100% of those questioned understood the word represented, always on the first try. A recurring observation was the fact that it requires some concentration to perceive it, however, it is always correct.
That being said, we made small adjustments to the letters that people found it most difficult to decipher. Without deconstructing the basic idea, in which the word was drawn only with geometric shapes, we used miniatures of them to create more characteristic and recognizable elements of the alphabet. Although subtle, these small openings (visible in the letters N, C and U) make reading easier, however, they allow us to maintain the initial aesthetic of the logo.

Our logo was built based on the Roboto font. We subjected each letter of the word Inclue to a geometrization process, using different geometric shapes that were complemented with slight transparencies and overlaps. Since the word itself already carries the meaning of inclusion, the set of different shapes gives the symbolism of union and strength, focusing on our main objective, which is raising awareness through an experience accessible to everyone. By adding these geometric elements to the selected typography, we give it a differentiating character that can also be transformed into graphic elements that can complement our identity in the future.

We subjected the logo to a color contrast test using the Chrome plugin Dalton. Thus, we obtained confirmation that there would be sufficient contrast between the selected colors so that they would be easily distinguishable by those suffering from color blindness. In the image above, we can visualize this color conversion, with the most frequent types of color blindness.

Style Guide

In order to appeal to a young audience, the image of our project is colorful and full of personality. Through blue we intend to convey the idea of security, stability and calm. Yellow contrasts with blue, due to its vivacity and energy. Purple symbolizes wisdom and knowledge and is used by brands that value human beings in general.
In this case, the choice of colors was essential for the general development of our project, since, according to our research, it would be extremely important to select suitable colors in terms of accessibility and play with contrast, making them more easily distinguishable.
The selected colors create a good contrast between each other, providing easy distinction between shades for those suffering from color blindness. Although less common among the different types of color blindness, the shade of purple we selected is the most visually contrasting color for those suffering from Tritanopia.
The font used in the logo, Roboto, will also be used on the website. Simple and sans serif, to facilitate reading for people with reduced vision, dyslexia or cognitive and neurological difficulties.

Test

Usability Test

In order to clarify doubts about our website, we carried out usability tests with four people. These interacted with what we can consider a demo version of the prototype, as it is still under construction.
Since our project is fundamentally narrative and exploratory in nature, we adapt usability tests to a simple opinion after browsing the website. Unlike the typical tasks to be performed, we leave the user completely free to explore, which allowed us to conclude how easily they navigate/find what they want, whether they understand the main objective of the project and what doesn't work or could be improved.
We also left it up to the user whether they preferred to comment during or after the test, although they could provide a more concise analysis at the end of the test.

Priority Revisions

Most frequent suggestions:

  • The navigation bar should not appear on the first page and make it clear that the project is an interactive documentary;
  • In terms of accessibility, it is important that there are more arrows indicating the path to follow and that the user understands what to do when they finish watching a video;
  • It is essential to find a solution for inserting videos on the website that brings some integrity to the documentary in visual terms. Youtube's recommendations, file names and starting frames do not allow the user to truly indulge in the interactive experience;
  • Change the thumbnails, which are all the same. If we change each thumbnail to the information that will be displayed, we create greater motivation and interest in the user;
  • Insert a clearer message into disability immersion videos, so that the user understands that, despite it looking like the same video, they need to watch it again;
  • André Almeida's page may have fewer videos;
  • Written information should be clearer overall and cohesive, including font size should be improved;
  • Present suggestions for improvements to digital accessibility in writing at the end of the experience.

Reflection & Next Steps

When we selected the concept of inclusion and accessibility for our project, we had the opportunity to delve into this topic and communicate with people who taught us a lot and made us think. In general, we are satisfied with the final result of the project, however we would like to see the final version of the website implemented, focusing on its interactivity, as it is not finished. If I were to push this project further, I would:

1. Re-test

Fix and retest the prototype.

2. Design Handoff

With a finalized version of the design, I would then present it to stakeholders and hand it off to developers to build it.
